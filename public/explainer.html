How this model works

A plain-language guide to the AI Energy Wall model: what it estimates, how it gets there, and how to read the results. No equations here. The maths live in the technical report.

The question

AI runs on electricity. As more people, companies, and software agents use AI, the electricity it needs grows. This model asks a simple question with big consequences: at what point does the electricity AI wants outrun what the Earth can supply it, and if a gap opens, could data centers in space help close it?

Nobody knows the answer with certainty, because it depends on assumptions about the future. So instead of giving one number, the model turns every assumption into a slider you can move, and shows you the consequences in real time. It is a tool for thinking, not a crystal ball.

How demand is built up

The model adds up AI electricity demand from three groups:

1. Consumers

Ordinary people using chatbots and assistants. Around a billion people do this today. Adoption follows an S-curve: slow start, rapid middle, then levelling off as it approaches the share of humanity that will ever use AI. This group is naturally limited by the number of people on Earth.

2. Corporate users

Knowledge workers using AI in their jobs. They use AI more intensively than casual consumers, so each one drives more electricity. This group is also limited by headcount, in this case the number of desk workers.

3. Autonomous agents

This is the one that breaks the ceiling. Agents are software that works on its own, around the clock, without a human watching each step. Crucially, agents are not limited by how many people exist. One person can set off dozens or hundreds of them. They run far more, and far heavier, AI work than any human typist could. In the long run, this group dominates everything else.

Because agents are the decisive factor, the model gives you the single most important dial: "agents per human", the number of active agents per person once the technology matures. At roughly one per person, AI stays a modest slice of the grid. At a dozen per person, AI can outgrow the entire planet's electricity supply.

The recursion wild-card.

Agents can spawn sub-agents, which spawn their own, like a tree of delegation. Each extra layer multiplies the number of running programs, so the electricity needed can compound exponentially. The model ships with this turned off, so it never inflates the central case, but you can switch it on (the "recursion depth" slider) to see how quickly things spiral if it takes hold.

Turning users into electricity

For each group the model multiplies the number of users by how many AI requests they make per day, and by how much electricity each request uses. It adds the overhead of running a data center and the cost of training the models. Two opposing forces shape the energy per request over time: hardware keeps getting more efficient (each request gets cheaper), while usage keeps getting heavier (models reason more per request). A single dial, the "Jevons rebound", lets you decide which force wins. Set to the middle, they cancel out.

What the Earth can supply

The model takes today's total world electricity generation and grows it at a rate you choose. But almost none of that is free: homes, industry, hospitals, and transport already consume nearly all of it. Electricity is used as it is generated, so today's grid is essentially fully spoken for.

So the model tracks non-AI demand separately. It grows with the population (more people, more homes and industry) plus a per-person electrification trend, so it responds to the population dial. The main chart shows it honestly: non-AI consumption as the base layer, with the three AI categories stacked on top, all measured against the line of total generation. A second chart breaks the AI portion into its categories on a log scale, since consumer and corporate use are tiny next to the non-AI base and would otherwise be invisible. There is no spare energy lying around waiting for AI; AI has to be added on top of everything the world already runs.

The breakeven

The moment that matters is when the whole stack, non-AI plus all AI, rises above the generation line: the year the world cannot generate enough to power both the rest of the economy and AI. From that point a gap opens between what is demanded and what the planet produces, and that gap is the market a new source of power, such as space, would serve.

Because non-AI demand already fills most of the grid, AI does not need to grow anywhere near the size of total generation to trigger this. It only needs to grow larger than the slice of new generation the world adds faster than ordinary demand rises. Whether AI eventually dwarfs the entire grid is a further, more extreme question, and it depends almost entirely on the agents-per-human dial and the recursion wild-card. The model does not assume it; it lets you test it.

Earth versus space

If a gap opens, you can close it two ways: build more on the ground, or launch data centers into orbit. Space has a real advantage, solar panels in the right orbit catch sunlight almost continuously and produce several times more power than on the ground. But getting hardware to orbit is expensive. The model compares the cost of both options and solves for the launch price at which space becomes cheaper than Earth. That break-even launch price is the number the whole space-data-center business case hinges on.

How to read the results honestly

  • What is robust: across almost any reasonable settings, total demand outruns generation sometime in the late 2030s, opening a large gap. That conclusion does not depend on cherry-picked numbers.
  • What is genuinely uncertain: whether AI ever outruns all of Earth's electricity. That hinges on deeply unknowable assumptions about agents, so the model treats it as an open question for you to explore, not a prediction.
  • The defaults are not a forecast. They are one defensible starting point. The real value is in moving the sliders and seeing what has to be true for each outcome.
